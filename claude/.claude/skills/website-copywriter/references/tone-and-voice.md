# Tone and Voice Guidelines

This guide helps match different brand voices when translating or creating website copy. Understanding a brand's voice ensures translations feel authentic and consistent.

## Understanding Voice vs. Tone

**Voice** = The brand's personality (consistent across all content)
**Tone** = The emotional inflection (varies by context and situation)

Example:
- **Voice**: Professional, helpful, knowledgeable
- **Tone** in success message: Warm, encouraging
- **Tone** in error message: Apologetic, solution-focused

## Formality Spectrum

### German Sie vs. Du

**Sie (Formal):**
- B2B professional services
- Financial/legal/healthcare
- Enterprise software
- Older/conservative audiences

**Du (Informal):**
- Consumer products for young adults
- Creative/lifestyle brands
- Very casual startups
- Community-focused platforms

**Mixed approach:**
Some brands use Sie but maintain a warm, friendly tone

---

### English Formality Indicators

**More Formal:**
- "We would be pleased to assist you"
- "Please do not hesitate to contact us"
- "We appreciate your business"
- Full sentences, no contractions

**More Casual:**
- "We're happy to help"
- "Feel free to reach out"
- "Thanks for choosing us"
- Contractions, shorter sentences

## Cultural Considerations

### German Markets

**Expect:**
- More detailed information
- Explicit data and specifications
- Privacy/security emphasis
- Direct communication (less "fluff")

**Avoid:**
- Over-promising
- Excessive superlatives
- Aggressive sales language
- Too much casualness in B2B

### English-Speaking Markets (US/UK)

**US Market:**
- More enthusiastic tone acceptable
- Benefit-focused over feature-focused
- Shorter attention spans
- Bold claims more acceptable

**UK Market:**
- Slightly more reserved than US
- Self-deprecating humor acceptable
- Less hyperbole
- Subtlety appreciated

## Brand Voice Checklist

When translating or writing copy, ask:

- [ ] What is the brand's primary archetype?
- [ ] What is the context of this specific content?
- [ ] Who is the target audience?
- [ ] What is the desired reader action?
- [ ] How formal should this be?
- [ ] What emotional response are we aiming for?
- [ ] Does this sound like the brand?
- [ ] Would a native speaker use these exact words?

## Adapting Voice Across Languages

Not every voice translates 1:1 between German and English. Here's how to adapt:

### Playful English ‚Üí German
English can be very playful; German playfulness is more subtle:
- Reduce exclamation points
- Keep humor but make it drier
- Maintain friendliness without over-enthusiasm

### Formal German ‚Üí English
German business formality can feel stiff in English:
- Maintain professionalism but add warmth
- Shorter, more active sentences
- Keep respect without stuffiness

### Innovation-speak
Both languages love tech buzzwords:
- English: More bold, transformative language
- German: Innovation balanced with precision
- Both: Explain benefits, not just features
